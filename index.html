Core Concepts

Everything starts here. These six ideas are the foundation of everything you'll learn about investing.

🏢
01 — WHAT IS A STOCK

Shares of Ownership

A stock is a small piece of a company. When you buy a share, you become a part-owner — entitled to a slice of the company's profits and growth.

🏛️
02 — THE MARKET

Where Stocks Trade

Stock exchanges like the NYSE and NASDAQ are organized marketplaces where buyers and sellers meet to trade shares. Supply and demand sets the price.

📈
03 — BULL vs BEAR

Market Moods

A bull market means prices are rising and confidence is high. A bear market means prices are falling. Both are completely normal parts of market cycles.

💰
04 — DIVIDENDS

Passive Income

Some companies share profits with shareholders through regular cash payments called dividends. It's like getting paid just for holding the stock.

⚖️
05 — RISK & RETURN

The Fundamental Trade-off

Higher potential returns always come with higher risk. Understanding your own risk tolerance is the first step to building a portfolio that works for you.

🧺
06 — DIVERSIFICATION

Don't Put All Your Eggs in One Basket

Spreading investments across many different stocks and sectors reduces your overall risk. One company falling doesn't sink your whole portfolio.

How the Stock Market Actually Works

Think of the stock market as a giant auction that runs Monday–Friday, 9:30am–4:00pm Eastern Time. Millions of buyers and sellers are constantly making offers, and prices update in milliseconds.

When more people want to buy a stock than sell it, the price goes up. When more people want to sell, the price drops. It's pure supply and demand.

  • Companies list their shares on an exchange via an IPO (Initial Public Offering)
  • After the IPO, shares trade freely between investors on the open market
  • The company only receives money from the IPO — not from day-to-day trading
  • Market makers ensure there's always a buyer and seller available
  • Prices reflect millions of people's collective beliefs about a company's future

How to Read a Stock Quote

Every stock has a ticker symbol — a short abbreviation (like AAPL for Apple) — and a real-time price. But there's much more to know when evaluating whether to invest.

  • Price: The last price at which a share traded
  • Market Cap: Total value of all shares combined. Large-cap = stable; small-cap = riskier but more growth potential
  • P/E Ratio: Price-to-Earnings — how much you pay for $1 of profit. High P/E = high expectations
  • 52-Week High/Low: The range the stock has traded in over the past year
  • Volume: How many shares traded today. High volume = high interest
  • Dividend Yield: Annual dividend as a % of stock price
  • EPS: Earnings per share — the company's profit divided by number of shares

Types of Stock Orders

When you're ready to buy or sell a stock, you don't just say "buy it" — you choose an order type that controls exactly how and when your trade gets filled.

Picking the wrong order type can cost you money. Here's what each one does:

  • Market Order: Executes immediately at the current price. Fast but no price guarantee.
  • Limit Order: You set the exact price you'll pay or accept. Only executes if that price is reached.
  • Stop-Loss Order: Automatically sells if the stock falls to a price you set — protects you from big losses.
  • Stop-Limit Order: Combines stop and limit — triggers a limit order when the stop price is hit.
  • Day Order: Valid only for the current trading day — cancels if not filled by close.
  • GTC (Good Till Canceled): Stays active until you cancel it, regardless of how many days pass.

// Order Type Quick Guide

MARKET

Buy/sell now at whatever the current price is. Best for liquid stocks when speed matters more than price precision.

LIMIT

Set your price. "Buy AAPL only if it drops to $170." The order waits patiently until your target is hit.

STOP-LOSS

Your safety net. "If AAPL falls below $160, sell automatically." Protects gains and limits downside without watching every minute.

💡 PRO TIP

Beginners should almost always use limit orders when buying. Market orders during volatile moments can execute at a much higher price than you expected — a phenomenon called "slippage."

Understanding Your Risk Tolerance

Before you invest a single dollar, you need to answer one critical question: How would you feel if your portfolio dropped 30%?

Your answer reveals your risk tolerance — and it should shape everything from what you invest in to how much cash you hold.

  • Aggressive: You're young, have long time horizons, and can stomach big swings. Heavy in growth stocks and small caps.
  • Moderate: Balanced approach — mix of growth stocks, dividend payers, and bonds. Can handle some volatility.
  • Conservative: Prioritize capital preservation. Focus on blue-chip stocks, dividend stocks, and bonds. Fewer surprises.
  • Time Horizon matters: Investing for 30 years? You can absorb short-term drops. Investing for 3 years? Be cautious.
  • Emergency fund first: Never invest money you might need within 1–2 years. The market can be down exactly when you need it.

Reading Candlestick Charts

Candlestick charts are the standard way to visualize how a stock's price moved over a period of time — each "candle" tells the story of a single day, hour, or minute.

Once you can read them, you'll be able to spot patterns that hint at where prices might go next. This is the beginning of technical analysis.

  • Each candle shows the Open, Close, High, and Low prices for a time period
  • A green/white candle means the price went UP during that period (close > open)
  • A red/black candle means the price went DOWN (close < open)
  • The "wick" or "shadow" shows how far price swung beyond the open/close range
  • Patterns like "Doji," "Hammer," and "Engulfing" signal potential reversals
  • Always combine candlestick signals with other analysis — don't rely on them alone

// Anatomy of a Candlestick

Green candle — Close was higher than open. Buyers were in control.

Red candle — Close was lower than open. Sellers were in control.

Upper wick — Price reached here but sellers pushed it back down.

Lower wick — Price dipped here but buyers stepped in to push it up.
